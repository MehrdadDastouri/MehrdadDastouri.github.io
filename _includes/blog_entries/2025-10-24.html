A Quick Look at the Beta Distribution

Understanding the "distribution of probabilities." | October 25, 2025

The Beta distribution is a fascinating continuous probability distribution defined on the interval $[0, 1]$. It is parameterized by two positive shape parameters, denoted by $\alpha$ and $\beta$. What makes it particularly powerful is its ability to model the uncertainty about a probability.

The probability density function (PDF) for a random variable $X \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$ is: $$ f(x; \alpha, \beta) = \frac{x^{\alpha-1}(1-x)^{\beta-1}}{B(\alpha, \beta)} $$ where $B(\alpha, \beta)$ is the Beta function, which serves as the normalizing constant: $$ B(\alpha, \beta) = \int_0^1 t^{\alpha-1}(1-t)^{\beta-1} dt = \frac{\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)} $$

Key Properties

The mean (expected value) of the Beta distribution is a beautifully simple formula: $$ E[X] = \frac{\alpha}{\alpha + \beta} $$ This intuitively makes sense: if you think of $\alpha$ as the number of "successes" and $\beta$ as the number of "failures" you've observed, the expected probability of success is just the ratio of successes to the total number of observations.

The variance is given by: $$ \text{Var}(X) = \frac{\alpha\beta}{(\alpha+\beta)^2(\alpha+\beta+1)} $$

Why It's Useful

In Bayesian inference, the Beta distribution is the conjugate prior for the Bernoulli, binomial, negative binomial, and geometric distributions. This means if you have a prior belief about a probability (modeled as a Beta distribution) and you observe new data (from a binomial experiment), your updated belief (the posterior) will also be a Beta distribution. This makes calculations incredibly convenient.
